# Diversifying a Concentrated PGR Stock Position With a Low-Cost Vanguard Five-Fund Portfolio

## Background and assumptions used in this report

You’ve accumulated a significant employer-stock exposure through PGR shares (including a large unvested component). Your goal is to reduce concentration risk over the coming years by selling some vested shares and reinvesting the proceeds into a diversified, low-expense portfolio—ideally using Vanguard index funds—while still targeting higher long-run returns appropriate for someone 15+ years from retirement and comfortable with equity volatility.

This report assumes the proceeds are investable for long-term goals (not needed for near-term spending), and it focuses on “what to buy with the proceeds” rather than the exact legal/tax execution of sales (which depends on your trading windows, equity plan type, and tax situation). ## Why a concentrated employer-stock position is a special risk

Holding a large position in a single company exposes you to **company-specific (“idiosyncratic”) risk** that a diversified index fund largely diversifies away. This is the basic diversification principle formalized in modern portfolio theory and supported by extensive research on how firm-level volatility contributes materially to the risk of individual stocks. citeturn8search5turn8search20turn23search0

A concentrated employer-stock position can also create a **“double exposure”** problem: your paycheck/career and your investment portfolio both depend on the same company’s fortunes. Government analysis of retirement-plan employer stock ownership has emphasized that, in severe adverse outcomes, employees can face simultaneous job loss and a large decline in employer-stock-based savings. citeturn21view0

Financial regulators frame this simply: concentration risk rises when too much of your portfolio depends on one investment, and broad diversification across asset classes and within stocks (by sector/issuer) is a primary mitigation tool—often best achieved via mutual funds or ETFs. citeturn7search1

## The five-fund portfolio framework and why it fits your goals

A “five-fund” design works well here because it can simultaneously deliver:
- **Broad global equity exposure** (your engine of long-run growth),
- **Geographic diversification** beyond the U.S. (useful when your concentrated stock is U.S.-listed),
- **A modest diversifier in high-quality bonds** (a volatility buffer and rebalancing “dry powder”),
- **A measured equity tilt** toward segments with higher expected returns in some forward-looking models (optional, and sized conservatively).

To anchor forward-looking expectations, Vanguard’s March 2026 market outlook (10-year nominal projections) shows:
- **U.S. equities:** ~3.9%–5.9% expected annualized return, median volatility ~15.2%.
- **Global ex-U.S. equities (unhedged):** ~4.6%–6.6% expected return, median volatility ~18.9%.
- **U.S. small-cap:** ~5.2%–7.2% expected return, median volatility ~19.8%.
- **U.S. aggregate bonds:** ~4.1%–5.1% expected return, median volatility ~6.3%.
- **Global ex-U.S. aggregate bonds (hedged):** ~4.5%–5.5% expected return, median volatility ~5.0%. citeturn9view0

## Allocation recommendation and a practical answer to “what should the proceeds be used for?”

### The baseline target allocation for new money

Given your time horizon (15+ years) and stated comfort with risk, a **90/10 stock/bond** target is a reasonable “aggressive but diversified” baseline. This aligns closely with how Vanguard’s target-date glide path describes younger/long-horizon investors: roughly **90% stocks** with “just enough bonds” to moderate severe drawdowns. citeturn19search1turn19search0

A recommended **five-fund target allocation**:

- **44% VTI** (U.S. broad market core)
- **36% VXUS** (international stock diversification)
- **10% VBR** (small-cap value tilt for potential long-run return enhancement; optional but sized modestly)
- **7% BND** (U.S. bonds)
- **3% BNDX** (international bonds, USD-hedged)

This is constructed by taking Vanguard’s illustrative 90% equity starting point (54% U.S. / 36% international; 7% U.S. bonds / 3% hedged international bonds) and dividing the U.S. equity sleeve into a broad-market core plus a controlled small-value tilt. citeturn19search1turn17view0turn16view2turn16view0turn15view2turn15view0

### How this interacts with your large PGR exposure

Because PGR is a single U.S. stock, most investors in your situation are already **implicitly overweight U.S. equities** (and exposed to company-specific events). Even if you *want* high equity exposure, you generally do not need *single-stock* concentration to get it. citeturn7search1turn7search14turn21view0

So the most practical answer to “what should the proceeds be used for?” is:

**Use proceeds from PGR sales to buy whatever portions of the five-fund portfolio you are currently underweight in (relative to your chosen target), with a strong bias toward building the international stock and bond sleeves first until your overall portfolio no longer “lives and dies” with PGR.** citeturn7search1turn23search0turn19search1

If you want a simple rule without calculating your full household balance sheet, here is a “de-concentration first” default for *ongoing* sale proceeds while PGR remains oversized:

- **50% VXUS**
- **20% VTI**
- **10% VBR**
- **15% BND**
- **5% BNDX**

This is still growth-oriented (70% stocks / 20% bonds in the *marginal dollars*), but it accelerates building diversifiers you likely lack if PGR is your dominant holding. It also lines up with the idea that international equities can be an important diversifier relative to a U.S. single-stock concentration, and bonds can provide a materially lower-volatility counterweight. citeturn9view0turn7search1turn7search14

### Why this allocation is a reasonable “return vs. diversification” compromise

The key trade-off is:
- **Expected return** generally comes from higher equity weight (especially broad equities and—if you choose—factor tilts). citeturn9view0turn20search4  
- **Risk control** comes primarily from diversification across issuers/geographies and adding lower-volatility bond exposure. citeturn9view0turn7search1turn8search5

Given Vanguard’s 10-year outlook, global ex-U.S. equities have a somewhat higher projected return range than U.S. equities, but also higher volatility, which is consistent with using VXUS as both a diversifier and a potential return enhancer versus a U.S.-heavy starting point. citeturn9view0

Meanwhile, bonds (U.S. aggregate and hedged global ex-U.S.) are projected to have meaningfully lower volatility than equities, supporting their role as stabilizers even in an aggressive portfolio. citeturn9view0

## Implementation considerations that materially affect outcomes

### Trading restrictions and structured selling

If you’re subject to blackout windows or have potential access to material nonpublic information, a pre-planned selling approach (often a Rule 10b5-1 trading plan) can help systematize diversification and reduce “timing stress.” The entity["organization","Securities and Exchange Commission","us securities regulator"] has updated and modernized Rule 10b5-1 requirements, including adding cooling-off periods and good-faith conditions for plans. citeturn10search0turn10search8

### Tax mechanics of equity compensation and why they matter for reinvestment

If your PGR compensation is in RSUs (common for “unvested shares”), taxation is typically:
- **Ordinary income when RSUs vest and shares are delivered**, similar to wages. citeturn8search3turn8search11  
- **Capital gains/losses after vesting** if you continue to hold and later sell (based on the difference between sale price and your post-vesting cost basis). citeturn8search3turn8search7

This affects “what to do with proceeds” in a very concrete way: part of proceeds may need to be reserved for taxes if withholding is insufficient, and your net investable amount should be invested according to the target allocation above. citeturn8search3turn23search2

### ETF versus mutual fund share class considerations

For taxable investing, ETFs are often used because they can be more tax-efficient in handling shareholder flows, potentially reducing capital gains distributions relative to mutual funds (though taxes depend on each fund’s activity and distributions). Vanguard’s investor education describes this general ETF tax-efficiency mechanism. citeturn23search18turn23search2

### Rebalancing discipline

Once proceeds are invested into your five funds, you’ll want to **rebalance** periodically so you don’t drift back into unintended risk (for example, equities running up and bonds shrinking, or international falling behind and becoming de minimis). Vanguard describes rebalancing as the process of adjusting the portfolio back to your preferred asset allocation when it has shifted. citeturn23search0

### Optional tax-smart “pre-sale” alternatives (only if relevant to you)

If your PGR position has large unrealized long-term gains and you are charitably inclined, donating appreciated shares directly (rather than selling and donating cash) can reduce or eliminate capital gains taxes on those donated shares while supporting giving goals.
